Central AC vs Mini-Split: Technical Comparison

Detailed technical comparison of central air conditioning and ductless mini-split systems, including efficiency analysis, installation complexity, performance characteristics, and regional optimization strategies.

Photo by [Photographer] on Unsplash - Central AC vs mini-split system comparison

System Architecture

Central Air Conditioning

Traditional ducted system with centralized air handler and condenser. Air distributed through ductwork to multiple zones. Requires extensive ductwork and return air pathways.

Ductless Mini-Split

Distributed system with outdoor compressor and multiple indoor evaporator units. Refrigerant lines connect components. No ductwork required, zoned cooling/heating per room.

Efficiency Comparison

SEER Ratings

  • Central AC: SEER 13-22 (higher potential with variable speed)
  • Mini-Split: SEER 16-30 (inverter technology, higher efficiency)

Energy Performance

  • Central AC: Full load efficiency, duct losses reduce effective SEER by 20-30%
  • Mini-Split: Inverter modulation, precise capacity matching, minimal distribution losses

Operating Costs

Mini-split systems typically 20-40% more efficient due to elimination of duct losses and precise zoning. Central systems can achieve similar efficiency with sealed ductwork and zoning dampers.

Installation Considerations

Complexity and Time

  • Central AC: 8-16 hours, extensive ductwork modification or installation
  • Mini-Split: 4-8 hours per zone, minimal structural work

Permits and Codes

  • Central AC: Building permits required, ductwork inspection mandatory
  • Mini-Split: Electrical permits only, refrigerant line inspection

Existing Structure Impact

  • Central AC: Requires attic/crawlspace access, soffit vents, return grilles
  • Mini-Split: 3" hole through exterior wall per indoor unit

Performance Characteristics

Zoning Capability

  • Central AC: Limited zoning with dampers, temperature variations common
  • Mini-Split: Individual zone control, precise temperature regulation

Air Quality

  • Central AC: Whole-house filtration, consistent air distribution
  • Mini-Split: Room-specific filtration, potential for inconsistent air mixing

Noise Levels

  • Central AC: Indoor handler noise, ductwork transmission
  • Mini-Split: Indoor units 19-49 dB, quieter operation possible

Cost Analysis

Initial Investment

  • Central AC: $5,000-$12,000 (equipment + ductwork)
  • Mini-Split: $4,000-$10,000 (per zone, scales with rooms)

Operating Costs

Mini-split lower operating costs due to higher efficiency. Central systems can be optimized with sealing and zoning upgrades.

Maintenance Costs

  • Central AC: Filter replacement, duct cleaning, higher maintenance
  • Mini-Split: Filter cleaning, line set inspection, lower maintenance

Reliability Factors

System Longevity

  • Central AC: 15-20 years compressor, ductwork indefinite
  • Mini-Split: 15-20 years compressor, line sets 20-25 years

Failure Modes

  • Central AC: Duct leaks, improper sealing, zoning issues
  • Mini-Split: Refrigerant leaks, drainage issues, line set damage

Application Suitability

Best Use Cases

  • Central AC: New construction, whole-house comfort, existing ductwork
  • Mini-Split: Retrofit installations, room additions, supplemental cooling

Limitations

  • Central AC: Ductwork complexity, sealing challenges, zoning limitations
  • Mini-Split: Aesthetic concerns, line set visibility, higher cost per BTU

Portland-Specific Considerations

Portland's moderate climate favors mini-split efficiency. High humidity enhances dehumidification benefits. Seismic requirements affect installation standards. Marine air corrosion impacts outdoor units.

Building code allowances for ductless systems. Utility rebates available for high-efficiency installations. Consider long-term maintenance and service availability.
