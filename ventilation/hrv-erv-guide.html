HRV and ERV Guide: Heat and Energy Recovery Ventilation Systems

Heat Recovery Ventilators (HRVs) and Energy Recovery Ventilators (ERVs) represent advanced ventilation technology that addresses the fundamental challenge of providing fresh air while maintaining energy efficiency and indoor air quality. These systems continuously exchange stale indoor air with fresh outdoor air while transferring heat and, in the case of ERVs, moisture between the air streams. Understanding the differences, benefits, and applications of these systems helps homeowners make informed decisions about improving their indoor air quality.

Understanding Heat Recovery Ventilation Technology

Heat Recovery Ventilators (HRVs) use a heat exchanger to transfer thermal energy between incoming and outgoing air streams without mixing the air. During winter operation, warm indoor air heats the incoming cold outdoor air, reducing the heating load on the HVAC system. In summer, cool indoor air pre-cools hot incoming outdoor air, reducing cooling loads. This heat transfer process can recover 60-95% of the heat that would otherwise be lost through conventional ventilation.

The heat exchanger core represents the heart of HRV technology. Most residential units use either aluminum plate-type cores or polymer membrane cores. Aluminum cores provide excellent heat transfer efficiency and durability but don't transfer moisture. Polymer cores offer good heat transfer while allowing some moisture transfer, creating a hybrid between HRV and ERV functionality.

HRV systems work best in climates with extreme temperature differences between indoor and outdoor air, making them particularly valuable during Pacific Northwest winters when maintaining indoor comfort while providing fresh air becomes challenging and expensive without heat recovery.

Energy Recovery Ventilation: Advanced Moisture Management

Energy Recovery Ventilators (ERVs) transfer both heat and moisture between air streams, providing more comprehensive control over indoor conditions. The moisture transfer capability makes ERVs particularly valuable in climates with high humidity or where precise humidity control is important for occupant comfort and indoor air quality.

ERV cores typically use hygroscopic materials that absorb and release moisture as air streams with different humidity levels pass through. During humid summer conditions, ERVs transfer moisture from incoming outdoor air to the drier outgoing indoor air, reducing the moisture load on air conditioning systems. In dry winter conditions, ERVs can transfer moisture from indoor air to dry incoming outdoor air, helping maintain appropriate humidity levels.

The moisture transfer function makes ERVs more complex than HRVs but provides superior control over indoor humidity levels. This capability is particularly valuable in homes with occupants sensitive to humidity variations or in climates where seasonal humidity swings create comfort and indoor air quality challenges.

Comparing HRV and ERV Performance

Heat transfer efficiency ratings for both HRVs and ERVs indicate the percentage of heat recovered from the outgoing air stream. High-efficiency units achieve 85-95% heat recovery, substantially reducing the energy penalty associated with providing adequate ventilation. However, real-world efficiency depends on installation quality, maintenance, and operating conditions.

Moisture transfer effectiveness in ERVs is measured separately and typically ranges from 50-85%. This measurement indicates how effectively the unit transfers moisture between air streams. Higher moisture transfer rates provide better humidity control but may not always be desirable, depending on climate conditions and indoor air quality goals.

Electrical energy consumption varies between units but typically ranges from 100-300 watts for residential applications. When evaluating overall efficiency, consider both the recovered energy and the electrical consumption. Most quality HRVs and ERVs provide net energy savings because the recovered thermal energy exceeds the electrical consumption required for operation.

Sizing and Capacity Considerations

Proper sizing ensures HRV and ERV systems provide adequate fresh air without over-ventilating spaces or creating uncomfortable drafts. Ventilation requirements are typically calculated based on ASHRAE Standard 62.2, which considers home size, occupancy, and local climate factors. Most residential units are sized to provide 0.35 air changes per hour or 15 CFM per person plus 3 CFM per 100 square feet of living space.

Oversized systems may short-cycle or create drafts, while undersized systems fail to provide adequate fresh air or effectively dilute indoor pollutants. Professional calculation considers factors like home tightness, existing ventilation rates, occupancy patterns, and local climate conditions to determine optimal system capacity.

Variable-speed ERV and HRV units offer flexibility to adjust ventilation rates based on occupancy, outdoor conditions, and indoor air quality needs. These systems can operate at lower speeds during mild weather and increase capacity when outdoor conditions are extreme or when higher ventilation rates are needed for indoor air quality maintenance.

Installation Requirements and Ductwork

Successful HRV and ERV installation requires dedicated ductwork systems that are properly designed, sealed, and insulated. Supply ducts should deliver fresh air to bedrooms and main living areas, while exhaust ducts should remove air from bathrooms, laundries, and kitchens where moisture and pollutants are generated.

Ductwork should be sized according to manufacturers' specifications and installed with minimal restrictions to ensure proper airflow. Flexible ducts should be fully extended and supported to prevent sagging or kinking that restricts airflow. Rigid metal ductwork typically provides better performance and durability than flexible alternatives.

External vents require weather protection and should be located to prevent short-circuiting between supply and exhaust air streams. Intake vents should be positioned away from potential contamination sources like driveways, garbage areas, or exhaust vents from other equipment. Proper vent termination prevents rain infiltration while allowing unrestricted airflow.

Controls integration allows HRV and ERV systems to work effectively with existing HVAC equipment. Simple installations may use standalone controls, while more sophisticated systems integrate with central HVAC controls or smart home systems to optimize operation based on occupancy, outdoor conditions, and indoor air quality measurements.

Maintenance and Performance Optimization

Regular maintenance is essential for maintaining HRV and ERV performance and indoor air quality benefits. Filter replacement represents the most frequent maintenance task, with intervals typically ranging from 3-6 months depending on local air quality and system usage. Clogged filters restrict airflow and reduce heat recovery efficiency while potentially degrading indoor air quality.

Core cleaning should be performed annually or as recommended by the manufacturer. Aluminum cores can typically be washed with mild detergent and water, while polymer cores may require more careful cleaning procedures. Some units have dishwasher-safe cores that simplify maintenance.

Condensate drain maintenance prevents water accumulation that can support mold growth or cause damage to equipment or building materials. Drains should be checked periodically and cleared of any obstructions. Some climates may require condensate drain heaters to prevent freezing during cold weather.

Performance monitoring helps identify when maintenance is needed and ensures systems continue providing expected benefits. Many newer units include filters change indicators, while smart controls may provide performance data and maintenance reminders through smartphone applications.
